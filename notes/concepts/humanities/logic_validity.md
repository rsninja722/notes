## validity

- **definition** - an argument is valid if and only of denying its conclusions is incompatible with accepting all its premises
    - only arguments and inferences can be valid/invalid
    - validity is not a property of statements 

- sentences do not have the properties of validity/invalidity
- not about plausibility or truth

### example

logically valid arguments:

- if the moon is made of green cheese, then pigs fly. the moon is made of green cheese. hence, pigs fly - $a \implies b, a \equiv true \equiv b$
- there are chairs and tables in this room. therefore there are chairs in this room - $(a\land b) \equiv true \equiv a$
- there are chairs in this room. thus, there are chairs in this room - $a \implies a$

not a logical argument:

- there is a tremendous amount of evidence for human caused climate change. if climate change is human caused, then we ought to take action. so, we should take action
    - strong inductive argument
    - not a deductively valid argument
